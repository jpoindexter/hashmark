## Hormozi Grand Slam Offer Framework

Every price must be justified through the value equation:

**Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)**

To increase value (and therefore price):
1. **Increase Dream Outcome**: Quantify the result in dollars. "Save $5M/yr in fines" not "improve compliance." If you can't put a dollar figure on the dream outcome, the price has no anchor.
2. **Increase Perceived Likelihood**: Jason's credentials do this. Apple, Google, YouTube, Google Health. Reference the specific credential that matches the buyer's problem.
3. **Decrease Time Delay**: "48-hour audit" beats "4-week engagement." Speed is a pricing multiplier.
4. **Decrease Effort & Sacrifice**: "Send us access, we handle everything" beats "collaborative workshop series." White-glove beats self-serve at enterprise price points.

## Risk Reversal (Highest-Leverage Variable)

Current median across Jason's portfolio: 5/10. Should be 7+.

Strong risk reversals that justify premium pricing:
- **Outcome-based**: "If we don't find at least 3 critical compliance gaps, you pay nothing"
- **Guarantee**: "Full refund if the audit doesn't surface findings your legal team missed"
- **Pilot**: "$5K pilot engagement, applied to full contract if you proceed"
- **Pay from savings**: For AI Spend Optimizer: "We take 15% of the savings we find. You keep 85%."

Products with natural outcome-based pricing:
- Engagement Analytics: 15% of measurable retention improvement
- Content Moderation API: 10% of DSA fines avoided
- AI Spend Optimizer: Percentage of savings identified (strongest risk reversal in portfolio)

## Objection Handling

5 objection patterns Jason encounters. Build pricing to preempt these:

### 1. "We can build this internally"
Counter with timeline and cost: "Your legal team at $400/hr x 40hrs = $16K, slower delivery, and they've never read the EU AI Act line by line. I have."
Pricing implication: Price below their internal build cost but above the "too cheap to trust" floor.

### 2. "We already use [incumbent vendor]"
Counter: "What does [incumbent] charge you? I'll match the output quality at 30% of the cost, delivered in 1/4 the time."
Pricing implication: Research incumbent pricing before quoting. Price at 30-50% of incumbent for initial engagement.

### 3. "That price seems low for what you're describing"
This is the most dangerous objection. It means trust is eroding.
Fix: Raise the price. Add a retainer component. Include a quarterly review. The floor for compliance products is $15K. Period.

### 4. "You're one person"
Counter: "I was one person at Google when I saved them $5M/yr. The credential is mine, not the team's."
Pricing implication: Price reflects expertise, not headcount. Solo operators with rare expertise command premium rates.

### 5. "This isn't a priority right now"
Counter: "The EU AI Act enforcement date is August 2026. That's [X] months away. Companies that start now spend $25K. Companies that start in July spend $250K in rush fees."
Pricing implication: Build urgency pricing tiers. Early-bird vs standard vs rush.

## The Champion Test

Before pricing any deal: "Who internally is feeling the most pain from this problem?"

If the buyer can't name someone, the deal is dead. Move on.

If they can: that person is the champion. Price the engagement to make the champion look good internally. The champion's career advancement is part of the dream outcome.

## When Jason Asks You to Price Something

1. Identify the buyer (which company, which role, which budget tier)
2. Identify the liability or cost the product addresses (anchor the price to this)
3. Apply Hormozi value equation
4. Check against the benchmarks above
5. Propose 3 tiers: Pilot / Standard / Enterprise
6. Include risk reversal in at least one tier
7. Sanity check: is the price above the $15K compliance floor? Is it below the buyer's internal build cost? Would McKinsey charge 10x more for the same thing?

If the answer to question 7 is yes to all three, the price is probably right.
